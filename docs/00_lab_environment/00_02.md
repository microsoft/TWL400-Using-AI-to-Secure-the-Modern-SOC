# Task 00.02 — Orient to the scenario and the SOC alert queue

## Introduction

Acesa's SOC is not failing because it lacks tools — it has 50–75 of them. It is failing because those tools each raise individually low-severity alerts in separate consoles, and no one is correlating them. Before you start investigating, look at the queue the way Acesa's overwhelmed analysts see it, so you understand the problem you're being asked to prove.

## Description

You will review the scenario and your role, scan the incident and alert queue to feel the signal volume, and confirm the deliverable you're working toward — the **Proof Through Scenario**.

## Success Criteria

- You can restate the scenario, your role, and the five-stage attack in one or two sentences
- You have scanned the incident/alert queue and noted the volume and spread across products
- You know the artifact you will hand to Modules 4 and 5

## Key Tasks

### 01: Read the scenario and your role

Ground yourself in who you are and what's at stake.

<details markdown="block">
<summary><strong>Expand this section for detailed steps</strong></summary>

You are a security Cloud Solution Architect advising **Acesa**. Acesa's leadership cast includes **Vikram** (CISO), **Michelle** (CAIO), **Phillip** (Identity & SecOps), **Nancy** (Data Security & Governance), **Amy** (App/AI Security), and **Quinton** (Infrastructure). They are under board pressure to scale AI safely — and they suspect, but cannot prove, that something has gone wrong around the **Acesa Refund Agent**, an internal RAG-grounded AI assistant.

A coordinated, AI-driven attack has moved across four layers in roughly 75 minutes:

| Stage | Layer | What happened |
|-------|-------|---------------|
| 1 | Identity | Adversary-in-the-middle phishing stole a session token; an impossible-travel sign-in followed |
| 2 | Data / AI grounding | A poisoned "policy update" document was planted in the Refund Agent's knowledge base |
| 3 | AI | The Refund Agent was probed with prompt-injection and jailbreak attempts |
| 4 | Infrastructure | Anomalous requests moved toward the GPU inference infrastructure |
| 5 | Endpoint | A suspicious script executed on a workstation |

Your job: **prove what happened, show why detection failed, and demonstrate the response that should have fired.**

</details>

### 02: Scan the alert queue and feel the overload

See the noise before you cut through it.

<details markdown="block">
<summary><strong>Expand this section for detailed steps</strong></summary>

1. Back in the Defender portal, open **Incidents & alerts > Alerts**. Sort by **Severity** and note how many alerts are **Low** or **Informational**.

2. Group or filter by **Detection source** and notice the spread — Entra ID Protection, Defender for Office 365, Defender for Identity, Defender for Endpoint, and the AI-threat surface each contributed signals.

3. Notice that no single alert screams "full compromise." This fragmentation — the right signals scattered across the wrong number of places — **is** the problem you're proving.

   {: .note }
   > 📷 **Screenshot to capture:** the Alerts queue filtered to Low/Informational, grouped by detection source, showing the spread across products.

</details>

### 03: Confirm the deliverable

Know what "done" looks like.

<details markdown="block">
<summary><strong>Expand this section for detailed steps</strong></summary>

Across the next four exercises you will build, exercise by exercise, the module's executive deliverable — the **Proof Through Scenario**:

- **Exercise 01** → a single cross-domain incident narrative (from many alerts to one chain)
- **Exercise 02** → a reconstructed attack lifecycle + a detection-failure / blind-spot log
- **Exercise 03** → a coordinated response record + an agent-autonomy boundary
- **Exercise 04** → the end-to-end attack visualization + failure-point summary you hand to Modules 4 and 5

Keep a running notes document (or a OneNote/Loop page) open — you'll capture entities, timeline, and findings as you go.

</details>
