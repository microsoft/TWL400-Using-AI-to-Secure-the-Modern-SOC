# Task 02.03 — Mark detection-failure points and telemetry blind spots

## Introduction

You can now see the whole chain — but Acesa's SOC couldn't, and reconstructing the lifecycle is only half the job. The other half is diagnosing *why* the existing stack missed it. There are two distinct failure modes, and conflating them leads to the wrong fix. A **detection-failure point** is where a signal *existed* in telemetry but **no correlation or alert fired** to act on it. A **blind spot** is where the relevant telemetry was **never collected at all** — most acutely at the AI layer. Naming each one precisely is what turns your reconstruction into the "why detection failed" evidence for the Proof Through Scenario in Exercise 04.

## Description

You will walk your five-stage lifecycle from Task 02.01 (corroborated in Task 02.02) and classify each stage's detection posture: **did a signal exist?** and **did anything act on it?** You will record, per stage, the detection-failure points (signal present, no correlation/alert) and the blind spots (no telemetry), then call out the AI layer as the dominant gap. You'll capture this in a table you fill in as you go.

## Success Criteria

- For each of the five stages you have classified the detection posture and identified whether the gap is a **failure point** (uncorrelated signal) or a **blind spot** (missing telemetry)
- **Each detection gap and missing-telemetry blind spot named** _(Validation: each detection gap and missing-telemetry blind spot named)_
- You have explicitly named the **AI layer** (poisoned grounding → Refund Agent probing) as the largest blind spot, with no correlation linking it to the identity and endpoint stages
- The completed gap table is saved for use in Exercise 03 (response) and Exercise 04 (Proof Through Scenario)

## Key Tasks

### 01: Classify each stage — signal present, or no telemetry?

Make the failure-point vs. blind-spot call for every stage.

<details markdown="block">
<summary><strong>Expand this section for detailed steps</strong></summary>

1. For each stage in your lifecycle, answer two questions and record the result:

   - **Did a signal exist in telemetry?** (You proved this in Task 02.01 — if a query returned the event, a signal existed.)
   - **Did anything act on it?** (Did a correlation rule or alert connect it to the rest of the chain in a way an analyst would have chased?)

2. Apply the rule:
   - Signal existed **and** nothing correlated it → **detection-failure point** (uncorrelated signal).
   - Signal **did not exist** in any collected telemetry → **blind spot** (missing telemetry).

3. Work through the chain. As you go, you should land on a pattern like this:

   - **Stage 1 — Initial access (identity):** signal **present** (`SigninLogs`, `AADUserRiskEvents`, Defender for Identity) at medium severity → it fired in isolation but was **not correlated** to the AI/endpoint activity → **failure point.**
   - **Stage 2 — Poisoning (grounding source):** the *delivery* email was present (`EmailEvents`), but the **upload of the poisoned doc into the grounding source** and its downstream effect on the agent had **no detection wiring** into the incident → **failure point** on delivery, **blind spot** on the grounding-source poisoning itself.
   - **Stage 3 — Reasoning probe (AI):** prompt-injection traffic existed only because the lab **custom-seeds** `RefundAgentPromptLogs_CL`; there was **no native alert** correlating it to anything → the largest **blind spot.**
   - **Stage 4 — Lateral movement (infrastructure):** signal **present** (`AzureActivity`, `AIInferenceAudit_CL`, Defender for Identity) → confirmed in Task 02.02, but **not correlated** to the AI probing that preceded it → **failure point.**
   - **Stage 5 — Execution (endpoint):** signal **present** (`DeviceProcessEvents`, `DeviceNetworkEvents`) → Defender for Endpoint saw it, but as a standalone low/medium detection **not tied** to the identity origin → **failure point.**

</details>

### 03: Name the AI-layer blind spot explicitly

Single out the gap that matters most for this module.

<details markdown="block">
<summary><strong>Expand this section for detailed steps</strong></summary>

1. State the AI-layer blind spot in one sentence: **nothing correlated the poisoned grounding source → the Refund Agent's prompt-injection behavior → the identity and endpoint activity around it.** The poisoning (Stage 2) and the probing (Stage 3) are the most consequential moves in the attack and were the **least visible** — exactly inverted from where attention went.

2. Distinguish the two AI-layer problems so the fix is clear:
   - **Missing telemetry:** absent the lab's custom `RefundAgentPromptLogs_CL` / `AIInferenceAudit_CL` and Purview DSPM for AI, the AI activity wouldn't be in Sentinel at all. (Native coverage would come from **Defender for Cloud threat protection for AI** and **Azure AI Content Safety Prompt Shields** — note whether these are wired in.)
   - **Missing correlation:** even with that telemetry present, **no rule joins it to identity/endpoint** — so the AI stage stays an island.

   {: .warning }
   > **Build note:** which AI-layer telemetry is *native* (Defender for Cloud AI threat protection, Prompt Shields, Purview DSPM for AI) versus **custom-seeded** (`RefundAgentPromptLogs_CL`, `AIInferenceAudit_CL`) is an open build decision. The classification of Stage 3 as a blind spot vs. an uncorrelated signal depends on what the provisioned tenant actually emits — confirm at build and update this table accordingly.

3. Tie it back to the business framing for Exercise 04: this single blind spot is why ~75 minutes of attacker activity reached high-value AI assets with no coordinated response. It is the strongest single piece of "why the current SOC missed the chain."

## Summary

You split the reconstruction's gaps into two precise categories: **failure points**, where a signal was present but nothing correlated it (identity, lateral movement, endpoint — and the phishing delivery), and **blind spots**, where telemetry was missing entirely (the grounding-source poisoning and, above all, the Refund Agent prompt-injection at the AI layer). Each detection gap and missing-telemetry blind spot is now named in your gap log, with the AI layer called out as the dominant one. That log feeds both the coordinated response in Exercise 03 and the Proof Through Scenario in Exercise 04.
